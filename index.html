### Activity 4: Create, Analyze, or Improve Your Intended Learning Outcomes

Summary:
Formulate, evaluate, and optimize your intended learning outcomes (or other types of goal descriptions, such as end terms or learning objectives) using taxonomies and/or SMART criteria.

Formulating clear, measurable, and effective learning outcomes is often an essential part of your educational design. Learning outcomes describe what students are expected to know or be able to do at the end of a lesson, module, course, or program. The better your learning outcomes are described, the easier it is to create matching activities and assessments (see Activity 3).

A poor learning outcome:
"Students understand how to write good emails."
This learning outcome is weak because it is not observable (how can you see 'understanding'?), lacks specificity (what does 'good' mean?), is not time-bound (when are emails considered good?), and is too vague (what type of emails?). This makes it difficult to determine whether students have actually acquired the intended skill.

A good learning outcome:
"By the end of this course, students can write a business email in English, using the correct tone and structure, and grammatically correct sentences at B1 level."
This learning outcome is good because it specifically states what students should be able to do ("write a business email in English"), clearly indicates the context ("using the correct tone and structure, and grammatically correct sentences"), and is measurable ("by the end of this course" and "at B1 level"). These criteria can easily be converted into an assessment rubric. Additionally, the target audience is mentioned, and the learning outcome includes a time indication ("by the end of this course").

Example Prompts:

Activity Example Prompt
Formulating learning outcomes "I am a [ROLE] in [CONTEXT]. My target audience consists of [TARGET AUDIENCE]. For a [LESSON, SUBJECT, COURSE] on [TOPIC], I want to formulate [NUMBER] learning outcomes..."
Improving existing learning outcomes "I am a [ROLE] in [CONTEXT]. My target audience consists of [TARGET AUDIENCE]. For a [LESSON, SUBJECT, COURSE] on [TOPIC], I have created the following intended learning outcomes: [LEARNING OUTCOMES]..."
Analyzing existing learning outcomes "I am a [ROLE] in [CONTEXT]. My target audience consists of [TARGET AUDIENCE]. For a [LESSON, SUBJECT, COURSE] on [TOPIC], I have formulated the following existing learning outcomes: [LEARNING OUTCOMES]..."

Activity 7: Create interactive learning activities

Summary:
Design engaging assignments that enhance the involvement and understanding of your students, such as discussion assignments, brainstorming tasks, icebreaker activities, formative assessments, and other creative ideas.

Engaging assignments are intended to enhance the involvement and understanding of students by actively involving them in the learning process. These assignments can take various forms, such as discussion assignments, brainstorming tasks, icebreaker activities, and formative assessment methods. By focusing on active cognitive processing, students achieve deeper learning. However, just because students are 'busy' does not necessarily mean they are cognitively engaged. Designing engaging assignments is one thing, ensuring that something actually happens in their minds is another. Regular checks for understanding are therefore essential, highlighting the importance of formative assessment.

Example Prompts:

Activity Example Prompt
Generating ideas for assignments "I am a teacher in [CONTEXT] and I teach the following target audience: [TARGET AUDIENCE]. I want to develop ideas for assignments for the following learning outcomes: [LEARNING OUTCOMES]. Provide 10 suggestions in bullet points for the following types of assignments: [DISCUSSION/BRAINSTORM/ICEBREAKER/ETC.]. Explain the advantages and disadvantages of each suggestion."
Elaborating on an assignment idea "Elaborate on the idea for an assignment about [IDEA NAME] in more detail. Pay attention to: [KEY POINTS]."
Writing instructions for an assignment for students "Write the instructions for [ASSIGNMENT]. My target audience consists of: [TARGET AUDIENCE]. Consider the following key points: [KEY POINTS]."

Tips and Variations:

  • When a particular assignment idea appeals to you, have the tool elaborate on it in more detail. Provide key points that you find important, such as classroom setup, difficulty level, or the amount of preparation the assignment may require.
  • Consider how you can use technology to enrich the assignments and/or enable differentiation.
  • Involve students in designing engaging assignments.
  • Vary the teaching methods and assignments to keep engagement high.
  • Experiment with the flipped classroom model, where students acquire knowledge before the lesson and use the lesson for deepening and applying that knowledge.
  • Use multimedia resources, such as videos, images, and interactive tools, to make the assignments more interesting and visual.
  • Ensure a good balance between individual assignments and collaborative assignments. Both forms contribute to active learning and offer different learning experiences.
  • Regularly evaluate the effectiveness of the engaging assignments. Ask for feedback from students and adjust the assignments as needed to optimize the learning process.
  • Don't ask for just one idea, but have 50 ideas developed at once. Get inspired!

Reform Assessments or Design an Alternative Assessment Form

Summary:
Reform traditional assessments into alternative, authentic assessment forms or design new assessment forms that emphasize deep learning and the application of knowledge in practice.

The traditional idea of assessment, often based on fact reproduction, has limitations and typically focuses on a one-time performance. Alternative assessment forms often focus on deeper and authentic learning. In these forms, students are exposed to more authentic problems and tasks that better reflect real-world scenarios. This approach requires students to integrate a range of knowledge, skills, and attitudes and encourages an iterative approach rather than a 'one-shot' approach. The focus shifts from performing to learning, emphasizing individual development and gaining success experiences, which enhances both motivation and learning outcomes. Use language models to transform existing assessments into these alternative forms, or simply get inspired by possibilities for alternative assessment forms.

Example Prompts:

Activity Example Prompt
Generating an alternative assessment form "I am a teacher in [CONTEXT] and my target audience consists of [TARGET AUDIENCE]. I want to transform the following assessment form into an alternative assessment form: [DESCRIPTION OF OLD ASSESSMENT FORM]. The intended learning outcomes of the assessment are [LEARNING OUTCOMES]. Design an alternative assessment form that is more authentic, addresses higher-order thinking skills (such as analyzing, evaluating, and synthesizing), integrates a range of knowledge, skills, and attitudes, and emphasizes development rather than one-time performance. Provide three suggestions first, and then ask me which one you should elaborate on in more detail."
Generating suggestions for assessment based on intended learning outcomes "I am a teacher in [CONTEXT] and my target audience consists of [TARGET AUDIENCE]. Based on the following learning outcome(s), provide 10 suggestions for assessment: [LEARNING OUTCOMES]. Ensure these suggestions are authentic, address higher-order thinking skills, integrate a range of knowledge, skills, and attitudes, and emphasize development rather than one-time performance."

Tips and Variations:

  • Support alternative assessment forms with clear rubrics or success criteria. This helps students understand what is expected of them and how they can succeed in the assessment.
  • Use other digital technologies to support alternative assessment forms, such as a digital portfolio or tools for digital assessment.
  • Allow students to design and develop their own alternative assessment forms.
  • Consider assessing at the group level instead of individually.
